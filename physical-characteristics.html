The sea otter is one of the smallest marine mammal species, but it is the heaviest mustelid.[12] Male sea otters usually weigh 22 to 45 kg (49 to 99 lb) and are 1.2 to 1.5 m (3 ft 11 in to 4 ft 11 in) in length, though specimens to 54 kg (119 lb) have been recorded.[23] Females are smaller, weighing 14 to 33 kg (31 to 73 lb) and measuring 1.0 to 1.4 m (3 ft 3 in to 4 ft 7 in) in length.[24] For its size, the male otter's baculum is very large, massive and bent upwards, measuring 150 mm (5.9 in) in length and 15 mm (0.59 in) at the base.[25]

Unlike most other marine mammals, the sea otter has no blubber and relies on its exceptionally thick fur to keep warm.[26] With up to 150,000 strands of hair per square centimeter (nearly one million per sq in), its fur is the densest of any animal.[27] The fur consists of long, waterproof guard hairs and short underfur; the guard hairs keep the dense underfur layer dry. Cold water is kept completely away from the skin and heat loss is limited.[24] The fur is thick year-round, as it is shed and replaced gradually rather than in a distinct molting season.[28] As the ability of the guard hairs to repel water depends on utmost cleanliness, the sea otter has the ability to reach and groom the fur on any part of its body, taking advantage of its loose skin and an unusually supple skeleton.[29] The coloration of the pelage is usually deep brown with silver-gray speckles, but it can range from yellowish or grayish brown to almost black.[30] In adults, the head, throat, and chest are lighter in color than the rest of the body.[30]

The sea otter displays numerous adaptations to its marine environment. The nostrils and small ears can close.[31] The hind feet, which provide most of its propulsion in swimming, are long, broadly flattened, and fully webbed.[32] The fifth digit on each hind foot is longest, facilitating swimming while on its back, but making walking difficult.[33] The tail is fairly short, thick, slightly flattened, and muscular. The front paws are short with retractable claws, with tough pads on the palms that enable gripping slippery prey.[34] The bones show osteosclerosis, increasing their density to reduce buoyancy.[35]

The sea otter propels itself underwater by moving the rear end of its body, including its tail and hind feet, up and down,[32] and is capable of speeds of up to 9 km/h (5.6 mph).[10] When underwater, its body is long and streamlined, with the short forelimbs pressed closely against the chest.[36] When at the surface, it usually floats on its back and moves by sculling its feet and tail from side to side.[37] At rest, all four limbs can be folded onto the torso to conserve heat, whereas on particularly hot days, the hind feet may be held underwater for cooling.[38] The sea otter's body is highly buoyant because of its large lung capacity – about 2.5 times greater than that of similar-sized land mammals[39] – and the air trapped in its fur. The sea otter walks with a clumsy, rolling gait on land, and can run in a bounding motion.[33]

Long, highly sensitive whiskers and front paws help the sea otter find prey by touch when waters are dark or murky.[40] Researchers have noted when they approach in plain view, sea otters react more rapidly when the wind is blowing towards the animals, indicating the sense of smell is more important than sight as a warning sense.[41] Other observations indicate the sea otter's sense of sight is useful above and below the water, although not as good as that of seals.[42] Its hearing is neither particularly acute nor poor.[43]

The sea otter has a metabolic rate two or three times that of comparatively sized terrestrial mammals. It must eat an estimated 25 to 38% of its own body weight in food each day to burn the calories necessary to counteract the loss of heat due to the cold water environment.[47][48] Its digestive efficiency is estimated at 80 to 85%,[49] and food is digested and passed in as little as three hours.[26] Most of its need for water is met through food, although, in contrast to most other marine mammals, it also drinks seawater. Its relatively large kidneys enable it to derive fresh water from sea water and excrete concentrated urine.[50]

A sea otter has two types of fur, the underfur and the guard hair. The shape of these different hair fibers connect to trap air between them. This allows them to maintain their body heat without the blubber other sea mammals use.[51]
